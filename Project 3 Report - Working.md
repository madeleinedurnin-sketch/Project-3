# Introduction

The rise of social media has fundamentally changed how audiences respond to sports content, transforming historically neutral athletic spaces into complex arenas for public discourse. This project examines the intersection of sports and politics in contemporary media, specifically focusing on how public figures increasingly blur the boundaries between entertainment, athletics, and political messaging. In an increasingly partisan United States, politics is often viewed as a taboo or highly sensitive topic in mixed company. As individuals have grown more outspoken—and at times hostile—about their political beliefs, the cultural expectation of keeping sports separate from politics is continually challenged. With the continued integration of social media into everyday life, politicians have become remarkably visible in the media content that athletes and fans actively create and consume.

Moments that deliberately blend sports and politics create entirely new digital spaces for public reaction. Donald Trump's guest appearance on golfer Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" YouTube series provides a compelling and unique case study of this phenomenon. It highlights how audiences interpret, debate, and negotiate this overlap through comment sections across different digital platforms. This case explores the ongoing debate regarding whether sports should remain isolated from political ideologies or if the two can maintain a productive, co-existing relationship. Analyzing highly outspoken audiences on both sides of the political spectrum provides authentic insight into how sport-political integration is received in real time.

Understanding these dynamics is critical for several reasons. Audience reactions directly shape how sports, media, and politics intersect in the modern era. These public responses influence athlete reputation, fan perception, and broader media narratives. While blended content that introduces political figures into sports entertainment can dramatically increase overall engagement and viewership, it simultaneously carries a high risk of deep polarization and backlash. Consequently, investigating this integration helps sports managers, athletes, and organizations understand the inherent risks and strategic opportunities of producing political content. Driven by these factors, this research specifically asks: How do audiences across YouTube, X (formerly Twitter), and Instagram negotiate and react to the blending of sports entertainment and political messaging in the context of Donald Trump's appearance on Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" series?

# Related Literature

**Theoretical Frameworks: Media Presentation and Public Debate**
This project connects to core course concepts surrounding media presentation, public debate, and how social media platforms facilitate distinct cultural behaviors and norms. Our analysis relies on two primary sociological frameworks: Framing Theory and Public Sphere Theory. These frameworks help establish a foundational understanding of how audiences interpret and interact with blended content in contemporary media ecosystems.

Framing Theory explains how the presentation of content inherently shapes audience interpretation. In the context of sports media, content is traditionally framed through a lens of athletic performance, skill, and entertainment. However, when political figures like Donald Trump are introduced, the frame inevitably expands or fractures. Audiences are forced to interpret the content—whether through a purely sports lens, a purely political lens, or a blended lens. This active negotiation of frames is central to understanding how a seemingly simple golf video can become a lightning rod for partisan debate.

Public Sphere Theory complements this by highlighting how social media functions as a modern space for public debate and opinion formation. Historically, the public sphere was rooted in physical community spaces. Today, the digital public sphere allows for immediate, unmediated interactions between strangers. Social media platforms like YouTube, X, and Instagram are not merely distribution channels; they are active environments where public opinion is forged, tested, and polarized.

**The Politicization of Non-Political Spaces**
A critical dimension of our research question involves understanding why audiences react so strongly to political messaging within sports. Wang et al. (2024) provide crucial context for this phenomenon by exploring the partisan politicization of non-political online spaces. Through their analysis of digital reviews, they discovered that political discourse routinely infiltrates explicitly non-political domains. As they note, their findings provide "alarming empirical evidence for the politicization of non-political spaces" (Wang et al., 2024, p. 1).

This directly connects to our examination of the "Break 50" YouTube series. A golf channel is historically viewed as an apolitical zone dedicated to leisure and sport. However, the introduction of a polarizing political figure immediately politicizes the space. Wang et al. (2024) argue that "with identity politics on the rise, this study sheds light on the importance of considering non-political online spaces for the study of political discourse" (p. 1). By analyzing the comment sections of Bryson DeChambeau's video, we are observing this exact phenomenon: an entertainment-focused ecosystem transforming into a battleground for political ideology and identity performance.

**The Phenomenon of the Celebrity Politician**
To understand the specific reactions to Donald Trump in a sports entertainment setting, we must contextualize his unique position in the media landscape. Street (2018) explores Trump's form of celebrity in politics, noting that it is widely assumed he "has cashed in his success on the reality show The Apprentice to secure political credibility and attention" (p. 3). However, Street argues that Trump's impact goes beyond simply leveraging fame.

Street (2018) suggests that "celebrity politicians like Trump act as stars, whether of reality television, rock music or film. They do not just resemble stars, they are them" (p. 3). This is vital for our research because it explains why Trump's appearance on a golf YouTube channel feels entirely congruent with his brand, yet highly disruptive to traditional sports media. Street further asserts, "This is evident in how they are represented, how they perform and how their ‘fans’ respond" (2018, p. 3). When audiences react to the Break 50 episode, they are not just reacting to a former president playing golf; they are responding to a superstar celebrity politician performing in an entertainment capacity. This dual identity makes the content inherently hybrid, prompting the blended framing we observe in the comment sections.

**Identity Conflict in Sport Fandom**
Finally, the intersection of these political and entertainment identities inevitably creates friction for the audience. Larkin (2025) examines this friction, noting that "despite the natural intersection of sport and politics, it remains an understudied topic in academic literature" (p. 177). Our project directly addresses this gap by analyzing how fans navigate their allegiances when these worlds collide.

Larkin (2025) explains that "identity conflict occurs when two salient identities are perceived as incompatible" (p. 177). For politically engaged sport fans—particularly those whose political ideologies oppose Trump's—his appearance on a beloved golf channel forces an immediate identity conflict. Are they a golf fan first, or a political partisan first? Larkin asks a crucial question: "Does political identity conflict have the potential to compromise the loyalty of sport fans?" (p. 177). Our analysis of audience reactions across platforms provides empirical data to answer this, showcasing how fans either abandon their sports identity to engage in political combat, or attempt to aggressively depoliticize the space to protect their fandom. As Larkin (2025) points out, "today, brands feel increasing pressure to take political stands," meaning athletes and content creators like DeChambeau are actively navigating the very identity conflicts that dictate modern fan loyalty (p. 177).

**Identifying the Gap**
While it is well known that the United States is highly polarized, our data bridges a critical gap in the existing literature. We bring together Wang et al.'s observations on the politicization of neutral spaces, Street's framework of the celebrity politician, and Larkin's concept of fan identity conflict. This project adds authentic, cross-platform insight into exactly how this sport-political integration is received (comparing Instagram, X, and YouTube) and what that reception ultimately means for athletes and organizations weighing similar political collaborations in a polarized era.
