# Introduction

The rise of social media has fundamentally changed how audiences respond to sports content, transforming historically neutral athletic spaces into complex arenas for public discourse. This project examines the intersection of sports and politics in contemporary media, specifically focusing on how public figures increasingly blur the boundaries between entertainment, athletics, and political messaging. In an increasingly partisan United States, politics is often viewed as a taboo or highly sensitive topic in mixed company. As individuals have grown more outspoken—and at times hostile—about their political beliefs, the cultural expectation of keeping sports separate from politics is continually challenged. With the continued integration of social media into everyday life, politicians have become remarkably visible in the media content that athletes and fans actively create and consume.

Moments that deliberately blend sports and politics create entirely new digital spaces for public reaction. Donald Trump's guest appearance on golfer Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" YouTube series provides a compelling and unique case study of this phenomenon. It highlights how audiences interpret, debate, and negotiate this overlap through comment sections across different digital platforms. This case explores the ongoing debate regarding whether sports should remain isolated from political ideologies or if the two can maintain a productive, co-existing relationship. Analyzing highly outspoken audiences on both sides of the political spectrum provides authentic insight into how sport-political integration is received in real time.

Understanding these dynamics is critical for several reasons. Audience reactions directly shape how sports, media, and politics intersect in the modern era. These public responses influence athlete reputation, fan perception, and broader media narratives. While blended content that introduces political figures into sports entertainment can dramatically increase overall engagement and viewership, it simultaneously carries a high risk of deep polarization and backlash. Consequently, investigating this integration helps sports managers, athletes, and organizations understand the inherent risks and strategic opportunities of producing political content. Driven by these factors, this research specifically asks: How do audiences across YouTube, X (formerly Twitter), and Instagram negotiate and react to the blending of sports entertainment and political messaging in the context of Donald Trump's appearance on Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" series?

# Related Literature

**The Politicization of Non-Political Spaces**
A critical dimension of our research question involves understanding why audiences react so strongly to political messaging within sports. Wang et al. (2024) provide crucial context for this phenomenon by exploring the partisan politicization of non-political online spaces. Through their analysis of digital reviews, they discovered that political discourse routinely infiltrates explicitly non-political domains. As they note, their findings provide "alarming empirical evidence for the politicization of non-political spaces" (Wang et al., 2024, p. 1).

This directly connects to our examination of the "Break 50" YouTube series. A golf channel is historically viewed as an apolitical zone dedicated to leisure and sport. However, the introduction of a polarizing political figure immediately politicizes the space. Wang et al. (2024) argue that "with identity politics on the rise, this study sheds light on the importance of considering non-political online spaces for the study of political discourse" (p. 1). By analyzing the comment sections of Bryson DeChambeau's video, we are observing this exact phenomenon: an entertainment-focused ecosystem transforming into a battleground for political ideology and identity performance.

**The Phenomenon of the Celebrity Politician**
To understand the specific reactions to Donald Trump in a sports entertainment setting, we must contextualize his unique position in the media landscape. Street (2018) explores Trump's form of celebrity in politics, noting that it is widely assumed he "has cashed in his success on the reality show The Apprentice to secure political credibility and attention" (p. 3). However, Street argues that Trump's impact goes beyond simply leveraging fame.

Street (2018) suggests that "celebrity politicians like Trump act as stars, whether of reality television, rock music or film. They do not just resemble stars, they are them" (p. 3). This is vital for our research because it explains why Trump's appearance on a golf YouTube channel feels entirely congruent with his brand, yet highly disruptive to traditional sports media. Street further asserts, "This is evident in how they are represented, how they perform and how their ‘fans’ respond" (2018, p. 3). When audiences react to the Break 50 episode, they are not just reacting to a former president playing golf; they are responding to a superstar celebrity politician performing in an entertainment capacity. This dual identity makes the content inherently hybrid, prompting the blended framing we observe in the comment sections.

**Identity Conflict in Sport Fandom**
Finally, the intersection of these political and entertainment identities inevitably creates friction for the audience. Larkin (2025) examines this friction, noting that "despite the natural intersection of sport and politics, it remains an understudied topic in academic literature" (p. 177). Our project directly addresses this gap by analyzing how fans navigate their allegiances when these worlds collide.

Larkin (2025) explains that "identity conflict occurs when two salient identities are perceived as incompatible" (p. 177). For politically engaged sport fans—particularly those whose political ideologies oppose Trump's—his appearance on a beloved golf channel forces an immediate identity conflict. Are they a golf fan first, or a political partisan first? Larkin asks a crucial question: "Does political identity conflict have the potential to compromise the loyalty of sport fans?" (p. 177). Our analysis of audience reactions across platforms provides empirical data to answer this, showcasing how fans either abandon their sports identity to engage in political combat, or attempt to aggressively depoliticize the space to protect their fandom. As Larkin (2025) points out, "today, brands feel increasing pressure to take political stands," meaning athletes and content creators like DeChambeau are actively navigating the very identity conflicts that dictate modern fan loyalty (p. 177).

**Identifying the Gap**
While it is well known that the United States is highly polarized, our data bridges a critical gap in the existing literature. We bring together Wang et al.'s observations on the politicization of neutral spaces, Street's framework of the celebrity politician, and Larkin's concept of fan identity conflict. This project adds authentic, cross-platform insight into exactly how this sport-political integration is received (comparing Instagram, X, and YouTube) and what that reception ultimately means for athletes and organizations weighing similar political collaborations in a polarized era.

# Data & Materials

**Dataset Selection and Context**
The primary data source for this project comprises user-generated comments collected from three distinct social media platforms: YouTube, Instagram, and X (formerly Twitter). All data is specifically tied to audience reactions to a single sport-political moment: Donald Trump's guest appearance on Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" YouTube series. Because the data isolates reactions to this specific crossover event rather than evaluating either figure's broader social media presence, it provides highly targeted, authentic insight into how audiences negotiate the deliberate blending of sports and politics. The raw datasets—`B50_INS_COMMENT.xlsx`, `B50_X_COMMENT.xlsx`, and `B50_YT_COMMENT.xlsx`—were acquired following the episode's release. The specific data collection windows reflect the immediate reactionary periods for each platform: July 23 to September 2, 2024 for Instagram; July 23 to August 1, 2024 for X; and July 23 to August 11, 2024 for YouTube.

**Volume and Platform Architecture**
The total volume of data analyzed in this study is massive, encompassing 58,464 total comments across the three platforms. Breaking this down by platform reveals significant variations in audience engagement architecture:

- **YouTube (45,623 comments):** Generating the vast majority of the data, YouTube's structure naturally encourages longer, essay-style replies and threaded discussions. This dataset heavily reflects how users deeply interpret the sports-political content, capturing detailed support, criticism, and neutral reactions.
- **Instagram (11,833 comments):** As a visual-first platform, Instagram drives direct audience interaction that tends to be more sports-focused and reactive. The comments here are frequently shorter, utilizing emojis and aesthetic reactions, resulting in generally less overtly political responses.
- **X / Twitter (1,008 comments):** Though the smallest dataset in volume, X fosters real-time, text-heavy public discourse. This platform is inherently more opinion-driven, reflecting immediate, argumentative reactions and highly polarized debate.
  By combining data from these three fundamentally different social ecosystems, the study benefits from a diversified pool of comment styles and cultural norms.

**Scope and Inclusion**
To capture the full spectrum of audience reactions and minimize researcher bias, the scope of the analysis was highly inclusive. The study incorporated all rows from each dataset; no platform-level rows were dropped during the final analysis. Even comments containing empty or blank strings were retained and coded by the default rule sets to ensure the integrity of the total dataset volume was maintained. This comprehensive inclusion allows us to analyze not just the vocal minority, but the broader distribution of reactions across the highly engaged segment of the audience.

**Data Cleaning and Harmonization**
Preparing the data for a cross-platform analysis required standardizing disparate data structures into shared analytic variables. During the data cleaning phase, the varying text columns from each platform (e.g., `text` for Instagram and YouTube, `contents` for X) were harmonized into a single, unified `comment_text` field. We also established a unified `platform` variable to track the origin of each comment.

Furthermore, platform-native engagement metrics—which vary wildly in structure—were carefully aggregated to compute a raw engagement score (`engagement_raw`). For Instagram and YouTube, this included likes and comment replies; for X, it included likes, reply counts, retweets, reference counts, and comment views. Because a single "view" on X is fundamentally different from a "like" on Instagram, these raw scores were then normalized using within-platform z-scores.

# Analytical Approach & Justification

**Mixed-Methods Coding Framework**
To comprehensively analyze how audiences negotiate meaning, this study employed a mixed-methods coding approach. Rather than relying solely on automated sentiment analysis—which often fails to grasp internet culture and niche social slang—we utilized a hybrid model. This consisted of an initial dictionary-based detection script, followed by context-sensitive social rules, and finally, iterative, user-guided calibration.

This approach systematically categorized each comment across two primary dimensions: **Political Stance** and **Interpretive Frame**.

* **Political Stance** was operationalized into three categories: `pro-Trump`, `anti-Trump`, and `neutral/non-political`. The base dictionary logic scanned for supportive cues, campaign slogans, and praise to classify comments as `pro-Trump`. Conversely, it scanned for critical cues, accusations of criminality, and explicit anti-campaign messaging to classify comments as `anti-Trump`. Comments lacking these specific triggers defaulted to `neutral/non-political`.
* **Interpretive Frame** assessed the primary thematic lens used by the commenter. It was operationalized as `sports/performance` by identifying golf and performance vocabulary, or `politics/ideology` by identifying election, office, or party terminology. If a comment contained keywords from *both* dictionaries, it was classified as `blended`.

To illustrate the exact lexical triggers used in the initial dictionary-based detection script, the following chart outlines the core keywords and regular expressions mapped to their respective analytical classifications:

| Analytical Category          | Classification         | Core Keywords & Lexical Patterns                                                                                                                                                |
| :--------------------------- | :--------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Political Stance**   | `pro-Trump`          | *maga, potus, trump 2024, vote trump, president trump, former president trump, go trump, team trump, thank trump, trump won, 45 & 47, 47th president, president 47, 47 again* |
| **Political Stance**   | `anti-Trump`         | *felon, rapist, racist, dictator, traitor, anti-trump, fuck trump, ban trump, no trump, never trump, criminal, do not vote trump, not voting for trump, domestic terrorist*   |
| **Interpretive Frame** | `sports/performance` | *golf, break 50, break 47, round, swing, driver, putt, putter, birdie, eagle, par, fairway, green, shot, game, play, score, course, bryson*                                   |
| **Interpretive Frame** | `politics/ideology`  | *trump, potus, president, former president, election, vote, maga, gop, democrat, republican, leftist, rightwing, liberal, conservative, political, 47th president*            |

*Note: The `blended` frame classification is computationally derived when a comment triggers both `sports/performance` and `politics/ideology` keywords simultaneously.*

This operationalization was critical. Because the research question centers on the *blending* of sports and politics, we needed a dedicated "blended" category to track users who actively merged the two lenses, rather than forcing them into a binary choice.

**Resolving Ambiguity Through User Calibration**
A major limitation of purely dictionary-based methods is their inability to parse implied meaning, complex irony, or highly specific cultural inside jokes. To address this, the analysis underwent two rounds of explicit user-calibrated ambiguity resolution.

During Round 1, we established specific heuristics: numbers like "47" or "45&47" were coded as `pro-Trump` even without explicit name references. Conversely, generic positive references to "president" or "American" without explicit Trump cues defaulted to `neutral`. When comments contained mixed positive and negative cues (e.g., "MAGA... domestic terrorists"), the negative cue superseded to capture sarcastic or critical intent (`anti-Trump`).

Round 2 further refined this contextual understanding. For instance, the phrase "Make America Great Again" was handled dynamically: if a golf or sports context was present, it was coded as `pro-Trump` and `blended`; if isolated, it was categorized as purely `politics/ideology`. Similarly, sports-adjacent praise like "GOAT" or "king," when lacking explicit political markers, was defaulted to a `blended` frame to reflect the hybrid nature of the praise. Finally, explicit logic was built to handle sarcasm markers (e.g., "lol," "sure," "/s") which are notoriously difficult for automated systems to classify accurately. To audit this process and ensure reproducibility, transition matrices were generated between passes to document the exact count and percentage of changed labels.

**Engagement Normalization and Justification**
A core component of our analysis involved measuring the magnitude of audience reactions. However, cross-platform comparability presents a significant challenge because engagement architectures differ wildly. An engagement metric indicating viral success on X may mean something entirely different on Instagram.

To resolve this, we first computed a raw score (`engagement_raw`) by aggregating the available platform-native signals: likes and replies for YouTube and Instagram, and likes, reply counts, retweets, reference counts, and comment views for X. To enable direct cross-platform comparison, we applied a logarithmic transformation (`log1p(engagement_raw)`) to manage extreme outliers, followed by a within-platform z-score calculation (`engagement_norm`). This methodological choice is highly justified as it standardizes the magnitude of engagement relative to each platform's unique baseline, ensuring that X's massive view counts do not artificially overshadow Instagram's metric structure in the final analysis.

**Statistical Testing Strategy**
The quantitative analysis relied exclusively on non-parametric statistical tests. Because online engagement variables are notoriously non-normally distributed—being highly skewed and heavily zero-inflated (where many comments receive zero replies or likes)—parametric tests like ANOVAs would violate core assumptions.

Instead, to assess categorical differences such as cross-platform stance distribution, frame distribution, and stance-by-frame associations, we utilized Chi-square tests of independence, supplemented by standardized residuals and Cramer's V to measure effect size.

To evaluate continuous engagement differences—such as whether blended comments generated more engagement than non-blended ones—we employed the Mann-Whitney U test paired with Cliff's delta for effect size. For comparing engagement across the three stances, we utilized the Kruskal-Wallis test, followed by post-hoc pairwise Mann-Whitney tests with Bonferroni corrections to prevent Type I errors.

**Qualitative Interpretive Layer**
Finally, to ground the statistical findings in the reality of the discourse, an interpretive qualitative layer was added. Representative comment excerpts were selected from within each `stance x frame` matrix cell. To ensure quality and relevance, this selection prioritized higher-engagement comments, removed duplicates, and explicitly favored comments with clear lexical alignment to their assigned category. This mixed-methods triangulation ensures that the statistical trends we report are directly tethered to the lived language of the fans.

# Results

The quantitative analysis yielded several findings surrounding platform distribution, frame-stance association, and engagement differentials.

**Stance and Framing Distributions Across Platforms**
Across all three platforms, `sports/performance` was the dominant interpretive frame, and the highest proportion of comments remained `neutral/non-political`. However, X (formerly Twitter) displayed a different distribution compared to YouTube and Instagram. A Chi-square test of independence revealed that X possessed a significantly larger share of both explicitly political stances and `politics/ideology` framing than the other two platforms. In terms of directional valence, `pro-Trump` frequency outpaced `anti-Trump` frequency across all three datasets.

**The Association Between Stance and Frame**
The data demonstrated a statistically significant association between political stance and interpretive frame. Neutral comments were overwhelmingly concentrated within the `sports/performance` frame. `Pro-Trump` comments were observed most frequently within the `blended` frame. Conversely, `anti-Trump` comments were most heavily concentrated in pure `politics/ideology` frames rather than the `sports/performance` or `blended` categories.

**Engagement Differentials**
A Mann-Whitney U test confirmed that `blended` comments—those containing both sports and political lexical cues—yielded statistically significantly higher normalized engagement than non-blended comments. Furthermore, a Kruskal-Wallis analysis of engagement by political stance, followed by post-hoc pairwise testing, revealed platform-specific patterns. On X, explicit political comments generated the highest engagement metrics. On YouTube and Instagram, comments classified under a mixed-sports framework performed the highest. Overall, explicitly political and blended comments achieved higher raw visibility metrics than neutral commentary.

# Ethical Reflection & Limitations

**Methodological Limitations**
While this study provides robust, cross-platform insights into the blending of sports and politics, several methodological limitations must be acknowledged. First, the dataset represents a static snapshot captured during the immediate reactionary window following the release of the "Break 50" episode. Online discourse around a polarizing political figure is highly fluid. Consequently, this study cannot capture how public perception or platform narratives evolve over time as the video is reshared or as the broader political news cycle shifts.

Second, analyzing the dataset relied heavily on dictionary methods and rule-based coding, which inherently struggle with the nuances of internet culture. Comments often employ implied meaning, deep sarcasm, and inside jokes that simple text classification can miss or miscategorize. For example, users frequently use sports slang to make political points, and vice versa. While user-guided calibration mitigated this, some level of ambiguity remains. Furthermore, cross-platform comparisons are inherently complex; the fundamental difference between an engagement metric on X versus an engagement metric on Instagram means we are comparing structurally distinct architectures, even after normalization.

**Sample Bias**
It is also critical to acknowledge sample bias. The data reflects only a highly engaged minority of users who felt compelled enough to leave a public comment. The reactions of the "silent majority" who merely watched the video without interacting remain unknown. Additionally, the baseline audience inherently skews toward existing golf enthusiasts and Bryson DeChambeau subscribers, meaning these reactions are not perfectly representative of the general public.

**Ethical Considerations and Privacy**
Because this research analyzes the deeply personal and often inflammatory political ideologies of private users, strict ethical boundaries were established. While the datasets consist of user-generated comments from publicly accessible posts on Instagram, X, and YouTube, they originally contained identifiable metadata. To treat the data confidentially and protect user privacy, identifying markers such as usernames were completely removed prior to analysis. This ensures that individual users cannot be targeted or doxed based on their political expressions within our dataset.

**Mitigating Harm and Researcher Bias**
Beyond data privacy, a major ethical challenge involved the risk of amplifying the inflammatory rhetoric or partisan vitriol present within the data. The intersection of politics and sport, especially involving a highly polarizing figure like Donald Trump, naturally elicits heated and sometimes toxic discourse. Generalizing these findings without caution could inadvertently reinforce negative stereotypes about golf enthusiasts or users of specific platforms.

This challenge was navigated by maintaining strict academic objectivity. The analysis was strictly focused on *how* audiences negotiate meaning and boundaries, rather than validating, judging, or giving an unfiltered platform to extreme political sentiments. Furthermore, we actively acknowledged the risk of researcher bias. Because political comments are subjective, great care was taken during the manual calibration phase to avoid projecting our own political leanings onto the thematic coding, ensuring the final interpretations remained firmly grounded in the data.
